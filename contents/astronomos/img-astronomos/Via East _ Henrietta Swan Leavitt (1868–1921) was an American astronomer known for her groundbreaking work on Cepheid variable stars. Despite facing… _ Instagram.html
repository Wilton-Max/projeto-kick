    Henrietta Swan Leavitt (1868–1921) was an American astronomer known for her groundbreaking work on Cepheid variable stars. Despite facing gender-based obstacles, she joined the Harvard College Observatory as a “computer” in 1895. Leavitt’s meticulous analysis of stellar photometry data led to her discovery of the period-luminosity relationship among Cepheid variables. Her groundbreaking finding, published in 1908, revolutionized astronomy by providing a method to measure cosmic distances accurately. By correlating the period of variability of Cepheids with their intrinsic luminosity, astronomers could calculate their distances from Earth. Leavitt’s work laid the foundation for the cosmic distance ladder, enabling scientists to measure distances to far-off galaxies and nebulae. Although her contributions were initially overlooked, her legacy has since been recognized, with lunar and asteroid features named in her honor. Leavitt’s story underscores the importance of perseverance and the significant impact of her pioneering research in advancing our understanding of the universe.
